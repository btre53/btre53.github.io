Structure the article in four acts — use h3 for each section header (the style.css h3 gives the right weight):

**Act 1: The Scam**
Explain: A fake website at aib.ie1.online impersonated the AIB bank login page. If you visited on a desktop browser, it returned a 404 error — nothing to see here. But if you visited from a mobile phone, it showed a convincing fake login form. Why the difference? The site was checking the User-Agent header — the label every browser sends to identify itself. Desktop browsers send one kind of label; mobile browsers send another. The scammers filtered on this to avoid detection. Their goal: steal AIB registration numbers (8 digits) and PAC codes (5 digits) from customers who thought they were logging into their real bank.

**Act 2: Understanding the Target**
Explain: To fight the scam, you first needed to understand what it was checking. Scraping lists of real mobile User-Agent strings from whatismybrowser.com — a public database of browser signatures — gave a pool of hundreds of real Android and iOS identifiers. The login endpoint was /files/action.php?type=login and it accepted four fields: ip (the visitor's IP address), ua (User-Agent), regNumber, and PACNumber. The scammers were logging IP addresses too, possibly to block requests from suspicious sources like security organisations.

**Act 3: Building the Ammunition**
Explain: Three ingredients for convincing fake submissions: (1) Random but plausible AIB credentials — registration numbers between 10,000,000 and 99,999,999, PAC codes between 10,000 and 99,999. (2) Spoofed Irish IP addresses — scraped a table of Irish IP ranges from nirsoft.net, then shifted each octet by a small random amount to generate plausible-looking IPs that trace back to Ireland. (3) Realistic User-Agent mix — 72% Android, 28% iOS, matching the real European mobile market.

**Act 4: The Counter-Attack**
Explain: Sent 4,000 POST requests to the scammer's endpoint, each with randomised fake credentials, a spoofed Irish IP, and a real mobile User-Agent. Added one more detail: a 24% chance of resending each request — because the scam site responded with "credentials incorrect, try again," apparently to harvest re-entries from confused victims. The result: for every genuine victim who fell for the scam, the database contained roughly 200 fake entries. Finding a real credential in that noise would take longer than the data was worth.

**Closing paragraph:** One-liner on what this demonstrates — curiosity + action when something feels wrong.
