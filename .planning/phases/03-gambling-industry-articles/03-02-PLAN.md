SECTION 1 (h3: "The Attention Problem")
Open by naming the real problem: most analytical organisations are not short of data. They are short of attention. Dashboards surface information, but they do not decide what to look at first. The hard part is prioritisation under time pressure — a trading floor at 8am, a risk desk with twenty live markets, a data team with twelve requests in the queue. The question that matters is not "what happened?" but "where should a human spend the next hour?" Frame this as a triage problem, not an analytics problem.

SECTION 2 (h3: "Step One: Compress")
Describe the first stage of the pipeline. Raw transactional data — individual bets, timestamps, odds — is too granular to reason about quickly. The first step collapses it into weekly aggregates per market slice: total turnover, realised margin (what actually happened), expected margin (what the prices implied should happen), and the deviation between the two. This compression step is not about losing information — it is about producing a table where every row can be compared to every other row on the same scale. Note the key judgement call: a 2% deviation on a slice turning over one million euros is more significant than a 20% deviation on a slice turning over ten thousand. Size and deviation both matter.

SECTION 3 (h3: "Step Two: Rank")
The second step is purely mechanical. Take the compressed table and order the rows by largest absolute deviation from expected, weighted by turnover. No interpretation. No explanation. Just ordering. The human analyst does not yet enter the picture. The value of this step is that it removes the implicit hierarchy that most teams operate by — seniority, recency, loudness in meetings — and replaces it with a single consistent signal. Whatever is at the top of the ranked list is where money is moving in an unexpected direction.

SECTION 4 (h3: "Step Three: Summarise")
Introduce the language model at this step only, and be precise about what it does and does not do. The ranked table is passed to a local language model — in this case llama3.3:70B running via Ollama — with a strict prompt. The model is instructed to write a short triage note covering three things: where the largest deviations are concentrated, what drill-down questions a human analyst should ask first, and which items are uncertain enough to warrant caution rather than action. The model is not given context about the business. It is not asked to explain why something happened. It is not asked to make recommendations. It behaves like a junior analyst told to summarise a table and flag what stands out, nothing more. Every statement in the output must be traceable to a row in the input table. The output is a starting point, not a conclusion.

SECTION 5 (h3: "Why It Works")
Explain the design principles behind the pipeline. It is conservative by design: the LLM cannot hallucinate facts because it is only allowed to report what is in the table. It is auditable: the human analyst always has the ranked table alongside the triage note and can verify every claim in seconds. It is fast: the pipeline runs in minutes and lands in Teams before the trading day opens. And it is honest about its own limits — it does not pretend to explain why a deviation happened, only that it happened and that it is large enough to look at first. That restraint is what makes it trustworthy. A system that confidently explains everything is usually wrong some of the time in ways that are hard to detect. A system that clearly states what it does not know is more useful for the same reason.

SECTION 6 (h3: "The General Lesson")
Pull the lesson out of gambling. Triage problems appear everywhere that humans must allocate limited attention across many signals: hospital emergency departments, security operations centres, customer support queues, content moderation teams. The compress-rank-summarize structure is general. The role of a language model in this structure is narrow and powerful — it converts a ranked table into readable natural language so a human can spend thirty seconds understanding what needs their attention instead of five minutes reading a spreadsheet. That is the right use of the technology. Not to replace human judgment, but to reduce the time it takes to apply it.
