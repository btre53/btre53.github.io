SECTION 1 (h3: "Scarce Space")
Introduce the resource allocation problem concretely. A bookmaker's homepage has a fixed number of featured slots — say, twelve positions where the most prominent events are displayed. Each week there are hundreds of events competing for those positions. Each event has a cost (editorial time to set up the feature, ongoing pricing attention during the event) and an expected return (additional turnover generated by the feature placement). If the options were completely independent and there were no interactions between them, the right answer would be straightforward: rank everything by return divided by cost and pick from the top until the slots fill up. The problem is that real options are not independent. Two football matches from the same league on the same day compete for the same audience. Featuring both costs more and returns less than featuring either one alone. That interaction is what makes the problem hard.

SECTION 2 (h3: "Why Greedy Fails")
Explain the failure mode of the naive approach. Sorting by return-to-cost and selecting from the top looks sensible and is easy to explain, but it breaks down in the presence of constraints. The algorithm is blind to interactions: it cannot see that the second football match on the same list will cannibalise the first, or that a different combination — slightly lower on the sorted list — would perform much better because the events complement each other. Greedy selection optimises items individually. The real problem requires optimising combinations. These are not the same thing, and the difference in outcome can be large when the constraints are binding.

SECTION 3 (h3: "What Optimisation Buys")
Explain what a proper optimisation approach does differently. Instead of evaluating items one at a time, it evaluates combinations — thousands of candidate combinations against the constraints simultaneously. It finds the combination that maximises total expected return while respecting all the capacity and interaction constraints. The output is not a recommendation. It is a baseline: here is what is achievable given your current constraints. That baseline is useful in three ways. It shows the true cost of each constraint (remove one constraint and re-run — the gap tells you exactly what that constraint is costing). It reveals trade-offs that were previously invisible (taking slightly less from event A frees capacity to include event B and C, net positive). And it gives stakeholders a concrete answer to "are we leaving money on the table?"

SECTION 4 (h3: "When You Do Not Need Optimisation")
Introduce classification as a deliberate alternative, not a fallback. For some decisions, optimisation is overkill. Tennis event featuring — should a given match be highlighted, listed as standard, or deprioritised? The relevant signal is "star power": the share of all historical bets on the platform that involved at least one of the players in the match. A single threshold rule works: above the threshold, feature it; below, deprioritise it. No prediction model, no combinatorial solver, no cleverness. Explain why this is a conscious choice rather than a limitation. The signal is interpretable — every stakeholder understands what star power means. The rule is consistent — the same logic applies to every match, no special cases. Exceptions are easy to argue: disagreements become concrete ("is star power the right signal for this?" or "should the threshold move for Grand Slams?") rather than vague ("the algorithm seems wrong"). Simple rules that are well understood beat complex models that nobody can interrogate.
