SECTION 1 (h3: "Odds Are Prices")
Reframe odds as probability claims. A decimal price of 2.0 means the event has a 50% implied probability. Formula: 1 divided by the decimal odds. Walk through two more examples: 4.0 implies 25%, 1.5 implies 67%. Bookmakers are not predicting outcomes — they are quoting prices that embed probability claims. That framing explains where the profit comes from.

SECTION 2 (h3: "Where the Margin Lives")
Explain the overround. On any event with two outcomes, the implied probabilities for both sides sum to more than 100%. The excess is the bookmaker's structural margin — say, 105% total means 5% baked in. On a single event the bookmaker can and does lose. The margin only materialises across thousands of events. Small edge, enormous scale: that is the whole model.

SECTION 3 (h3: "Calibration")
Introduce calibration. Take all events ever priced at 50% implied probability. How often did they actually happen? If the answer is 50%, the prices were accurate. If the answer is 43%, there is a systematic error. A calibration curve plots implied probability (x-axis) against observed frequency (y-axis). Perfect calibration sits on a 45-degree diagonal. Deviations reveal where prices are systematically wrong.

SECTION 4 (h3: "The Favourite-Longshot Bias")
Describe the persistent pattern: at short odds, the calibration curve is close to fair. At long odds, events occur measurably less often than their prices imply — longshots are systematically overpriced. This has been documented across horse racing, football, and other sports markets for decades. Two contributing factors: behavioural (people overvalue the excitement of a long-shot ticket) and structural (bookmakers have less information on unlikely outcomes and shade prices conservatively). The bias is where a disproportionate share of the structural margin actually lives.

SECTION 5 (h3: "The General Lesson")
Lift the lesson out of gambling entirely. Insurance companies price the probability that a claim will be filed. Credit analysts price the probability of default. Warranty providers price the probability of product failure. The calibration curve is the same diagnostic in every case: plot predicted probability against observed outcome rate. If the curve bends, there is a systematic error. Finding and correcting that bend is the core of the job. At scale, small systematic mispricings compound into large losses — and small corrections compound into large competitive advantages.
